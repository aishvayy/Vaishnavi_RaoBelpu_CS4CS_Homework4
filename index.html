I will write a paragraph for each methodology of SDLC. There are many of them but I will suggest a few. My suggestion and reasons are as follows:

(a) The Waterfall Model: Although not very widely used in software/product development these days, I have included the Waterfall Model as it is the fundamental SDLC model from which all other SDLC models have evolved. The disadvantages are often talked about, the biggest being that it leaves very little to no room for changes once a particular phase is completed, rendering it unflexible. I will discuss its nature and advantages which have made it so popular in its early days of use. The Waterfall Model is named thus because of the linear and logical manner in which projects that follow this model proceed, resembling the way water flows off cliffs in a waterfall. As pointed out earlier, there is rarely any scope for change or client/end user feedback in the multiple phases of a waterfall model - this means that requirements must be clearly defined initially, while also paying specific attention to good documentation, the cornerstone of any good product. Projects adhering to the Waterfall Model are very well documented which make it an attractive option. A common feature of the Waterfall Model which makes it a good choice for projects with a huge team size and ample resources is its clearly defined steps. Each phase of the Waterfall Model depends solely on the output of the previous phase, thereby making project management easier on a high level for complicated projects to simplify end goals. Based on all the previously described points, it follows that projects that choose the Waterfall Model are always well-structured, have barely any change in expectations versus the final outcome, and are very suitable in places where a fixed timeline for the project is of utmost importance, along with the need for an application to work out of the box at the first try. All the advantages also pose as disadvantages as projects following this model are at risk of losing customers.

(b)The V Model: The V Model, sometimes referred to as the "Verification" or "Validation" model, is thus named for the structure of its different phases from requirements gathering to module design and coding, each of which is associated with its own testing phase, which form two legs of the letter 'V'. This consistent testing ensures that money allocated to the project is utilised effectively, as major errors and issues are addressed at every phase before the start of the next one. Such a model also improves the group dynamics of a project by achieving an efficient level of collaboration between the development and testing teams, which makes it an incredibly attractive option for senstive projects like defense and aerospace research. The V Model gives a disciplined approach to project management while also providing quality assurance of the end product because of the high emphasis on constant testing and feedback from the testing team.

(c) The Spiral Model: In the third model of software development, I will be shedding light on the Spiral Model, a risk-driven SDLC. A combination of the Waterfall and the Iterative Models, this SDLC methodology lays special emphasis on risk management through its many phases. It is called a Spiral Model because of its diagrammatic representation of a spiral, each loop pertaining to one particular phase of the project. The number of phases of this SDLC are dynamic and determined by the manager, with continuous risk analysis and feedback from the clients that are subject to changes. With this kind of flexibility, it is well suited for large projects that come with high levels of risk. The spiral is divided into four quadrants, with the radius at any point depicting the cost incurred and the angular dimensions represent the progress made. With one spiral depicting the step-wise planning nature of the Waterfall Model, the repeated spirals depict further iterations to refine the process and come back to redefining some core characteristics of the project based on the risk analysis.

Among so many SDLC methodologies, the ones that are in the Agile classification are better. Agile SDLC model offers a flexible and iterative approach to software development, promoting continuous collaboration, rapid delivery, and responsiveness to change. Among Agile SDLC methods, I have the following suggestions:

(a) Scrum Methodology: One of the newest SDLCs catered to the increasingly fast-paced world of technology we inhabit today, the Agile SDLC was created with a mindset to approach software development in small, manageable iterations called "sprints". Each sprint comes with its own set of deliverables, which include a recurring phase of requirements gathering, refining, development, testing, incremental release of features of the product. The sprint lasts an average of two weeks, with enhanced communication between various teams - from security to product to development to testing, and each meeting referred to as a "scrum", where the team gets together to discuss the deliverables of the sprint/week/day, depending on what suits their needs. This not only improves the group dynamics of a team, but also facilitates quick project completion with an eye for customer satisfaction as end-user testing at the end of every sprint calls for reviews from the client and incorporating them into the following sprint.

(b) Crystal Agile Methodology: This is a lightweight Agile framework best suited for small projects with developers working out of a single workspace to large projects with an expanded team size. It is also divided into different colour codes like Crystal Orange, Crystal Diamond, Crystal Maroon, etc., based on the nature of risk to human life in the project. Some colours correspond to large team sizes such as Crystal Diamond and Sapphire, and others such as Crystal Clear are for small teams of 1-6 members. This method allows team members to take more control and freely exchange ideas within the team for the benefit of the product and user satisfaction.

(c) Feature Driven Development: This Agile methodology was first applied in the year 1997, and involves first creating an overall model, then developing the model feature-by-feature. It is best suited for small iterations and therefore suitable for complex projects where the product is launched efficiently, is feature focused, and customer oriented.

(d)Dynamic Software Development Method: This Agile methodology is based on the principle that 80% of any product can be delivered in 20% of the overall time set aside for the project. This means that in every iteration, just enough work is done in building and shipping the features on top of which the other features are built in subsequent iterations.

(e) Extreme Programming: This model of Agile is customer-first. It involves implementing features based on "User Stories", where features are described by product managers based on user requirements. Intensive coding follows this, which has intra-team code reviews, testing (both unit and integration), and also Continuous Integration - where all code is maintained and shared on a common repository. Paired with scrum, this often produces very efficient results in the development of a software.

I have used CSS on my webpage. The three ways to include CSS in a webpage are as follows:
(a) External CSS: This method allows us to include CSS for the entire webpage in a single, separate file. This is added by including a link to the location of the CSS file (with a ".css" extension) in the "link" element inside the "head" section of the HTML. For the purposes of this webpage, an external CSS has been used. For example: <link rel="stylesheet" href="styles.css">
(b) Internal CSS: This method of styling is used if a unique style is added to the HTML webpage, and is included by writing all the required styles enclosed within the "style" tags of the "head" section of the HTML code. For example: <style> body { background-color: lightblue; } </style>
(c) Inline CSS: Inline CSS is mainly used when we need to add styles to a singular element of the webpage, be it an article, or a section. To include inline CSS, we simply add the "style" attribute with any HTML element. For example: <h1 style="color: red;">Welcome to My Webpage</h1>

The reasons CSS is used in a webpage are as follows:
(a) CSS ("Cascading Style Sheets") are a way for us to design our webpages. It allows us to add any number of unique styles to our website, thereby making it visually appealing.
(b) CSS allows us to control how we want our website and the HTML which is the skeleton of the site to look on a browser.
(c) CSS allows us to present our website in an easy to understand format by the end user, by adding structure to our site and paint the layout in an artistic manner.
(d) CSS can be used to add effects such as animations to our website, or for simple things like text styling as well. This enhances the design of the website, similar to how a work of art becomes attractive when we paint the basic layout with colours.

We also use JavaScript in webpage development. The main reasons are as follows:
(a) JavaScript is one of the 3 main languages of web development, the other 2 being HTML and CSS.
(b) JavaScript allows us to program the behaviour of a website.
(c) JavaScript's utilities include dynamically updating the content of a website, control multimedia, and implement other complex features.
(c) HTML and CSS allow us to define the structure and design of a website - JavaScript controls everything else, from security to displaying interactive maps. Basically, everytime our website does something more than just displaying information to the user, JavaScript is what allows this interaction. This can be something like defining what happens when a user clicks on a button that triggers another action.
(d) JavaScript enables storage of information inside variables, operations on pieces of texts (commonly referred to as strings), and essentially, allows a website to come to life and makes it useful to a customer. When a user places an order on a shopping site, it is JavaScript that contains the logic of how a cart is updated or how the user's choice of product is stored.
